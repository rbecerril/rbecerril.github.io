Rafael is an Associate Professor at the University of South Carolina.

He investigates the overall and disparate effects of socioeconomic factors (e.g., income inequality, socioeconomic status) and technology (e.g., computer algorithms) on consumer behavior, business decisions, and market outcomes.

His goal is to design economically-sustainable business policies that balance business profitability, social welfare, and fairness.































Research

I investigate the overall and disparate effects of socioeconomic factors (e.g., income inequality and socioeconomic status) on consumer behavior (e.g., purchase incidence and brand choice), business decisions (e.g., assortment and pricing policies), and market outcomes (e.g., product sales and product availability).

My current and recent projects belong to one of the following groups:

Changes in the distribution of income are reshaping markets, leading consumers to adjust their consumption according to their new levels of purchasing power. This in turns leads manufacturers and retailers of consumer products to change their products and their assortments.

In a first project, my coauthors and I analyzed seven years of sales of consumer packaged goods across 944 product categories and across the U.S. to find that increasing income inequality leads to smaller assortments in the mainstream grocery channel. We investigate three potential demand explanations and find that the effect is mostly the result of the income losses of the middle-income households (consistent with Engel's Law of quantity).

In a second project (work in progress), my coauthor and I investigate the role of Permanent Income in outcomes of relevance to marketers (e.g., market basket size and composition). We find that using permanent income, as opposed to reported income, allows us to uncover income effects that are significantly larger than those previously documented in the literature.

A third study (forthcoming) proposes a data-collection methodology to explore whether and why algorithmic decision making in online retailing induces disparate impact. I build a multi-threaded webcrawler equiped with anti-detection techniques to collect pricing data for a random sample of zipcodes in the US. Then I match prices to local consumer demographics and find that prices differ across socioeconomic and ethnic groups. Finally, I explore the causes of these disparities.

Data to appear here soon.

An important effect of socioeconomic and technological change on consumer markets happens through social processes. Socioeconomic and technological changes transform the way people communicate and how they interact and this impacts their consumption.

In a first project, derived from my doctoral dissertation, I explore how the prices the different products of a brand (i.e., automobile make) affect the sales of all products of the brand. Among other findings, I show that the price of the entry-level product, if set low, helps the sales of the luxury brands but hurts the sales of non-luxury This is likely because low prices harm the prestige of non-luxury brands but make luxury-brands more affordable, and justifies common concerns in the automobile industry.

In a second study (work in progress), my coauthor and I propose a method to quantify how much households use different product categories to signal wealth using expenditure data.

In a third study (work in progress), I quantify the wealth-signaling effects of prices, finding that they are significantly larger than previously measured effects of positional concerns. I also find that consumers often counter-signal wealth to be perceived as warm instead of competent.

In online markets as well, socioeconomic and technological changes transform what consumers can afford as well as the way people inform their choices.

In a first study, my coauthor and I look into what determines the profitability of contingent free shipping offerings (free shipping for orders above a certain dollar amount). We find that, among all shipping-related costs, the fixed shipping fees may have the largest impact. In addition, retailer profits are higher the less heterogeneous consumer budgets are.

In a second study, we show extend the results to include inventory decisions and find that: (i) a reduction of the profit margin does not significantly affect the standard deviation of consumers' order sizes but increases the average order size; and (ii) variations in the free-shipping-qualifying order size affect both the average value and the standard deviation of the order sizes.  

In a third study (work in progress), my coauthors and I investigate the effect of consumer interactions on online retailing outcomes through consumer-generated ratings. In particular, we study the value of publishing customer delivery ratings in online market places and find that the most profitable policy can only be publishing true delivery performance or no delivery performance information at all. Publishing consumer-generated ratings tends to be suboptimal. We use a large sample of transactions provided by Cainiao to support our model.

Manufacturers and retailers often partner to offer hybrid goods (combinations of products and services), but this may generate tension between partners when post-purchase services are managed by retailers because different aspects of service may benefit manufacturers and retailers differently. My coauthors and I study how the market shares of new vehicles and dealer services depend on consumer satisfaction with service facilities and services staff.

We find that above-average satisfaction with staff boosts service shares but harms new vehicle shares. Above-average satisfaction with facilities hurts service shares. Moreover, these effects differ for mainstream and premium brands because (we conjecture) buyers of premium brands exhibit, on average, higher incomes and thus higher sensitivity to the time spent in having their vehicles serviced. The results were published here.

Do business firms benefit from promoting their products after a competitor suffers a major product recall? While some managers believe such recalls are opportunities to steal market share, others fear promotions may tarnish their brands. In our study, my coauthors and I assess whether car makers responded to 2010's Toyota's recall with promotions, whether the responses were effective, and whether the effectiveness varied across brand tiers.

We find that the Toyota recalls induced competitive promotions of approximately $850 on average. While 50% of premium brands increased promotions, only 36% of nonpremium brands did so. Among premium brands, 86% benefited from the promotional reactions but the returns for most nonpremium brands were nonsignificant or even negative.

Contribution

Research

I work on projects that are relevant to businesses, consumer welfare, and policy makers. In particular, my projects lay foundations for the evaluation of managerial decisions, consumer decisions, and public policies.

My aim is to generate new knowledge to support the improvement of both consumer welfare and businesses profitability.

Teaching

I teach courses on data analytics and data science, emphasizing industry's best practices and a think-first-then-do approach.

"Every battle is won before it is ever fought (Sun Tzu)."

With a solid understanding of data and methods, my students learn to pursue long-term competitive advantage while identifying potential biases and detrimental byproducts of data-based solutions.

Service

Involvement in multiple committees have allowed me to contribute to my workplace at multiple levels. Mostly, I serve in initiatives related to data analytics and computing.

More broadly, I serve as a reviewer for multiple journals in Marketing, Operations, Information Systems, Transportation, and Computer Science.
